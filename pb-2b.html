Sandy Schoettler

In my class on computing over encrypted data, we read a journal article for class called “Replication is Not Needed: Single Database, Computationally-Private Information Retreival.” Before I can explain what that means, there’s some context to cover. People are starting to become more concerned about security. But the interest in rigorous security among experts in the field itself has been an intense focus for years. A contingent of research has been focused on the idea of accomplishing tasks without actually requiring computations to use un-encrypted data. Surprisingly, many tasks can be accomplished in this way. The genre of this paper is research articles for doing various tasks in a “privacy-preserving” manner, meaning that they aim to show methods for doing these tasks in a way that provably cannot expose sensitive data. For example, the task of “Information Retrieval” is one involving two parties, say Alice and Bob. Bob keeps a list of values, indexed x_1, x_2, ... etc. Alice wants Bob to send her one of the values, but its important to her that Bob not know which value she wanted. Its okay if Bob knows she’s getting some value from his list, but she wants to keep the index a secret. Prior to this article’s publishing, the best solution to this problem was to have Bob’s list stored in 2 copies on different servers, where the security mainly stemmed from the requirement that the 2 servers hosting the list not be allowed to communicate with one another. The purpose of this article was to introduce a new method which only requires one computer on Bob’s end, meaning Alice no longer has to worry about the risk of colluding servers in the prior scenario. This task is called “Private Information Retrieval,” and the security of the procedure for accomplishing this paper’s solution stems from an assumption that a particular mathematical problem is difficult to solve quickly. The main argument in the paper is that a certain math problem can only be solved efficiently if some other information is already known. And since only the user (Alice) knows the information, it is overwhelmingly likely that Bob cannot ‘guess’ the information himself. And in a nutshell, they argue that this protects Alice’s privacy. Evidence in papers like this one include mathematical proofs, in this case based in number theory, other mathematical arguments involving probability theory (to prove that certain situations are to some extent ‘unlikely’), and often times empirical data testing the performance of an algorithm, though that is not used here. Often times the security of algorithms presented in articles in this genre rely on an assumption that a specific computation is hard to solve efficiently. When such an assumption is used by many articles over many years, with no apparent progress in finding efficient solutions, the hypothesis that that assumption is reasonable is repeatedly reaffirmed, simply by withstanding attempts to contradict it. In theory these are fundamentally assumptions and it is conceivable that other mathematical or computational techniques could be invented to break them, but standing the test of time often counts as robust evidence in the field. There is jargon abound in the genre, and this paper is no exception. The stated “Main Result” of the paper is that “For every c > 0 there exists a single-database \texttt{cPIR} scheme with communication complexity O(n^c), assuming the hardness of deciding quadratic residuosity.” There are plenty of terms referencing other algorithms in prior work, and mathematical concepts which are used frequently. This raises the barrier of entry to understanding the field but also reaffirms that the audience is specifically people in and peripheral to the field who understand those concepts. This article was relevant to the course because in the protocol, the user (Alice) sends Bob an encrypted version a request, and Bob uses that encrypted information to generate a response which he returns to Alice. But during the whole process, Bob never learns exactly what data Alice requested. So this algorithm is a great example of computing over encrypted data, which is what Bob does in this protocol.

The goals of the discourse community, and security research specifically, are to develop ways of performing tasks that people and users expect from services now, but to achieve those same applications in ways that can robustly protect users privacy. As security flaws have shown in recent years, data security and privacy are serious concerns, many of whom might not even know that their data might be at risk. But at the same time, the trend in this space of technology and in common culture is that people are becoming increasingly aware of and concerned about their data privacy, which fuels this active area of research with continued motivation and purpose.
