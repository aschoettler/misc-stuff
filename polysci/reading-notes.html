II - Institution

Institutions Structure Politics

Main Idea:

  • Institutions are routinized, structured relations which allow gov actors to address recurring problems or facilitate pursuing goals
  • They organize political activity

Terminology relating to structure of some institutions:

  • Jurisdiction: The domain where an institution / member has authority
    • ex FDA has jurisdiction / authority over marketing drugs.
  • Agenda Power: Power to control which items are put to a vote
  • Veto Power: power to override a vote
  • Decisiveness rules: A specification of when a vote may be taken and the sequence in which votes occur
    • ex the Senate rule that says a vote can be forced without further debate with 60 votes.
  • Delegation
    • Transmission of authority to some other official or body
    • ex president can delegate to cabinet officials
  • Principals & Agents
    • A principal can delegate to an agent to do some task
    • Challenge for leader: (1) make sure follower has aligned motives, (2) don’t expend too much cost policing delegated decisions.
    • ex Representative Democracy

Chapter 2 - The Constitution

Federalism and Anti-Federalism

  • Shays’ Rebellion, 1787
    • some people in Massachusetts didn’t want state legislature to pass some new legislation that would put them in debt. So they terrified them by trying to take over the national munitions @ springfield. Within a few days the Fed. had regained control of situation.
    • Demonstrated that the Articles of Confederation weren’t meeting needs, so Alexander Hamilton’s Annapolis Resolution was passed by the representatives at the whatever event.
    • Resolution said every state must send delegates to Philadelphia where they will plan better things.

Motivation for Redesigning the Articles of Confederation:

  • Wanted protection for commerce and property against radical state legislatures.
    • ship piracy for southern states
    • debtor revolt for larger states
  • Resist populist interests hostile to elites

First Constitutional Convention, 1787

  • Delegates from every state (except Rhode Island)
  • Virginia Proposal: originally proposed bicameral gov structure
  • Great Compromise: Agreement between larger & smaller states to have representation in Fed that was bicameral – divided into two chambers - where larger states had proportional representation in the house and the smaller states got equal rep. in the senate.
    • The two chambers had different powers

Three Fifths Compromise

  • Northern states: didn’t want slaves to count for representation
  • southern states: wanted slaves to count for representation
  • -> Three Fifths compromise for determining legislation (slaves still not allowed to vote).

Legislative Branch - Article I

Structure:

  • House of Representatives
    • Members elected by direct elections
    • Representation proportional to state population
    • Terms: 2 years
    • Goals:
      • directly responsible to the people
    • Powers:
      • Originate legislation about finance & commerce
      • change structure of federal courts
  • Senate
    • Members (originally) appointed by state leg.
    • Representation equal for each state
    • Terms: 6 years & staggered
      • avoid complacence in quick changes of public opinion.
    • Goals:
      • guard against excessive democracy
    • Powers:
      • Approve treaties
      • Approve federal judges

Powers of the Legislative Branch:

  • Express Powers
    • declare war, levy taxes, regulate commerce and currency, …
  • Necessary and Proper Clause A1.8
    • Power to enact laws necessary & proper to implement powers given to congress OR any other branch / office of the Fed.

The Executive Branch - Article II

  • Hamilton wanted “energy”

Goals:

  • Make Fed the agency for timely and decisive action to deal with public issues and problems
    • Hamilton called this “energy”
  • Withstand excessively democratic pressures by doing indirect elections. Powers:
  • Accept ambassadors
  • negotiate treaties
  • grant pardons, except in cases of impeachment
  • appoint major departmental personnel (ex FBI Director)
  • convene congress in a special session
  • Veto congressional enactments
    • can be overridden by two-thirds majority

Judicial Branch - Article III

Goals:

  • Enhance power of Fed
  • check radical democratic impulses
  • guard against interference w/ liberty and property from the rest of the gov. Supreme Court:
  • Powers:
    • determine whether a power was usable by: (1) Fed only, (2) Fed & states, or (3) states only.
    • determine whether a law is constitutional
    • resolve disputes between state & federal courts
    • resolve cases between citizens of different states
  • Structure
    • Justices have lifetime appointments
    • Justices cannot have salaries reduced while serving

Chapter 3 - Federalism and the Separation of Powers

Some relevant principles for context:

  • Federalism - Principle of division of governance into federal and state levels.
    • Idea is that states retain some power as entities themselves. This allows them to retain some rights & authority over themselves AND limits the potential power of the Fed.
  • Separation of Powers - Division of power of the federal government into 3 branches

Chapter 4 - Civil Liberties

Where do Civil Liberties come from?

Civil Liberties: (ch4) Limits on what people may do freely in society.

  • They are restrictions on gov, so the limit the [#collective] decisions people make via congress.
  • Bill of Rights (Liberties) [1791]
    • many provisions
    • [M1] Speech, press, religion, assembly
    • [M2] Bear arms
    • [M3] Soldiers
    • [M4] Search & Seizures
    • [M5] Due Process, double jeopardy, self-incrimination
      • Miranda rights ([sc][1966] Miranda v. Arizona)
    • [M6] Trial by Jury
    • [M7] $20
    • [M8] Cruel & Unusual Punishment
    • [M9] Not exhaustive
    • [M10] Other rights reserved to the States

HIstorical things:

  • Religion and the Establishment Clause

    • Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances.

    • Jefferson “Wall of Separation” (of church & state)
    • But some things have persisted, unchallenged by courts: money
    • Court struck down bible reading in public schools: “highly suggestive”
    • Court agreed that withholding money to a sports team b/c of religion was “viewpoint discrimination”
    • “under God” - added in in [1954]
      • upheld inconclusively in [sc]
    • 10 commandments issue: same justice made swing vote both ways [LGSA.115]
    • Thing: Religion cannot be part of any test required to hold office, BUT many citizen voters may choose not to vote for a particular candidate for any reason they want.
    • Time / Place / Manner regulation - [sc][1940] Cantwell v. Connecticut
    • Court struck down a prison beard ban because it violated a man’s right to express his religion by growing a beard
    • Court held that when A&F refused to hire a Muslim woman because her head scarf violated company dress code, they were engaging in religious discrimination.

Notable Cases in Civil Liberties History

  1. [sc][1971] Lemon v. Kurtzman
    • Lemon Test - The national or sate governments MAY make a policy dealing with religion IF:
      • policy must have a Secular Purpose,
      • primary effect neither advance nor prohibit religion, AND
      • govt doesn’t become excessively entangled w religion
    • ex: Laws to force religion to be taught in school is NOT ok because it fails 1 and 3.
  2. [sc] Miller v. CA
    • Miller Test - The government MAY restrict “obscene” speech, defined as speech which:
      • appeals to a prurient interest (according to contemporary community standards),
      • depicts sexual or excretory conduct in a patently offensive way; AND
      • taken as a whole lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value.
    • Two exceptions to Obscenity Standard:
      • minors do not have full [M1] rights; their exposure to non-obscene material MAY be regulated.
      • broadcasting is a special form of communication that is due weaker [M1] protection; because of inadvertency and children’s access.
        • different from, say, printed material because if you pick up a magazine/book/tape to read/listen to it, you are “opting-in” to consuming that content. If you don’t like it you can choose to stop. But radio / cable news must have restrictions because listeners don’t know what’s coming / can’t disengage as easily.
  3. [sc] Brandenburg V. Ohio - “Imminent Lawless Action”
    • The government MAY regulate speech that speech is “directed to inciting or producing imminent lawless action and is likely to incite or produce such action.”

Chapter 5 - Civil Rights

What are Civil Rights?

Civil Rights:

  • Requirements on government and organizations regarding treatment of people, especially equal treatment
  • Rules that govern collective decision-making processes and outcomes
  • They do extend to private actors
  • Types
    • some govern who can be involved in [#collective] decisions & political processes and how
      • ex: Who can vote, serve in office, have a trial, or serve on jury
    • some concern how people are treated in organizations and in society - ex: access to public facilities, schools, hospitals

Examples of Civil Rights [LGSA.149]

  • Political
    • Voting (18+ y/o citizens)
    • citizenship
  • Legal
    • due process (everybody)
    • habeas corpus - right to see a court if charged w crime (everybody living on US soil)
  • Social
    • right to not be enslaved
    • marriage rights
    • right to work
    • own property
    • education
    • medical care @ emergency rooms
    • standard of living
    • due compensation if Gov takes property

Principles

  1. Civil rights should be Universal. - apply to everybody.
  2. Civil rights should be Equal - all people with a right should have equal ability to practice it.

Compare/Contrast Civil Liberties & Civil Rights

  • in common:
    • restrictions on gov power
  • Differences:
    • CL makes provisions about people, regarding what people are allowed to do
      • protect peoples’ individual freedoms against potentially over-reaching government
    • CR makes restrictions on organizations, regarding how they can treat different groups of people
      • protect peoples’ treatment, access, and participation in context of potentially discriminating organizations or institutions

Relevance

  • US provides civil rights many other nations don’t have
  • When injustices need fixing, new civil rights can be extended.
  • Its up to people / processes to determine if/how to extend civil rights.

Requirements on government (and be extension private actors) to provide equal treatment of people

  • regulate who can participate and how (juries, voting, due process)
  • Rules that Gov must follow regarding treatment of people

Where do civil rights come from?

  • Federal Constitution
    • [A1.2] representation in congress, who can become president
    • [A1.9] prevents unlawful detention for all people
    • [1865][m13]
      • prohibited slavery
    • [1868][m14]
      • Equal Protection, Citizenship, and life, liberty, property
      • transformational
      • still used today for expanding civil rights
    • [1870][m15]
      • voting rights for African-American men
    • [1919][m19]
      • voting rights for women
  • Laws
    • [1964] Civil Rights Act
      • outlawed (race/color/religion/sex/origin)-based discrimination in employment
    • [1965] Voting Rights Act
      • Jim Crow No Mo
      • black & minority voting rights
      • outlawed southern states’ discrimination in voting laws, like literacy tests & poll taxes.
  • Judicial Rulings / Supreme Court
    • [1954][sc] [Brown v. Board of Education]
      • Banned “separate but equal” policies in Plessy v. Ferguson
    • [2015][sc] Obergefell v. Hodges
      • gay marriage rights
  • Executive Orders
    • [1942] Exec Order 8802 (FDR): banned racial discrimination in the defense industry
    • [1948] Exec Order 9981 (Truman): banned racial discrimination in the military
    • [1961] Exec Order 10925 (Kennedy): established affirmative action by government contractors
    • [1965] Exec Order 11246 (Johnson): banned discrimination by government contractors
    • [1993] Defense Directive 1304.26 “Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell” (Clinton): some tolerance for gays and lesbians in the military
    • [2012] DACA (Obama): quasi-legal status to immigrant children

History of Civil Rights

  • Founding (narrow)
    • Equality for White Men.
    • Const did not have right to vote
    • Very diff. treatment of various groups, allowed by congress
      • ex: women: no voting, limited access to education
      • ex: minority groups: no voting, no property rights
      • ex: Restrictions on black people - no civil rights
      • ex: 1857 Dredd Scott: Pro-slavery
    • rights tied to property ownership
      • rights to own property also limited to white men
  • Post Civil-War (1861-65): (Amendments 13, 14, 15)
    • [1865][m13]
      • prohibited slavery
    • [1868][m14] Equal Protection
      • transformational
      • still used today for expanding civil rights
    • [1869][m15]
      • voting rights for blacks
      • limited access - Jim Crow
  • [1870s]-[1880s] Jim Crow laws
  • [1896][sc] Plessy v. Ferguson - upheld state-sponsored segregation
  • [1919][m19] Women voting rights
  • [1954][sc] Brown v. Board of Education - segregation of schools unconstitutional
  • [1964] Civil Rights Act & [1965] Voting Rights Act
    • final end of Jim Crow
    • End of segregation
  • Today
    • Civil Rights granted to all Citizens
    • still in development
    • conception of civil rights has been shaped by [#historical] dev. of ideas and their interpretation by [#SCOTUS]
