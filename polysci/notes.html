Limits on Liberties

  • Religion and the Lemon test
    • [A1] Congress shall make no law …respecting the establishment of religion or prohibiting the free exercise thereof.
      • ex
        • displaying 10 commandments
        • teaching intelligent design
    • Lemon v. Kurtzman:
    • The national or state governments MAY make policy dealing with religion IF:
      • the policy has a secular purpose
      • the primary effect is neither advancing nor inhibiting religion
      • the government does not become excessively entangled with religion
    • ex school prayer:
      • fails 3 because of adjudicating use of different prayers.
    • ex illegal substance use for religion:
      • The drug law: passes 1, law passes 2, and 3.
    • ex teaching intelligent design
      • primary purpose is religious, so it fails 1. So teaching cannot be mandated
  • Free Speech
    • Brandenburg Test:
      • Brandenburg v. Ohio, 1969.
      • There is a difference bt:
        • expression of ideas and statement of thoughts, which are protected
        • fighting words or incitement to imminent lawless action, which are not protected.
      • ex Fighting words case 1942. Person can be punished if speech would lead to imminent lawless action eg assault
    • only restrictions are outcome-based, relating to lawless action.
    • govt allergic to restrictions that are content-based.
    • Hate speech might be inflammatory / fighting words in some cases but in general its protected
    • Gray areas: burning a cross on a yard: not protected
      • suggestion of moving nazi/far-right/white nationalist rhetoric, bend brandenburg.
    • Exceptions and the Miller Test:
      • Miller v. California.
      • sexually explicit material may be restricted iff it is obscene
      • The govt may restrict “obscene” speech, defined as:
        • appeals to a prurient interest (according to community standards)
          • internet
        • depicts sexual or excretory conduct in a patently offensive way
        • taken as a whole lacks serious literary, artistic, political or scientific value.
      • ex comedian prosecuted for obscenity. Radio != print. Court reasoned that content restrictions on radio should be more restrictive.
      • ex KKK - march and then stray peacefully. Ok at the time
    • Pause about far-right language.
    • conservatives on other side of social order – what?
  • Abortion
    • 4 cases to know.
      • Griswold vs Connecticut
        • Contraception: laws banning contraception up through 1960s.
        • Connecticut: people arrested in clinic.
        • argued BR implies a zone (penumbra) of privacy, protecting people against ban on contraception
          • Otherwise state would require a lot of information about the person from the state.
        • Majority Opinion:
          • A1, 3, 4, 5: penumbra.
        • Concurring opinions:
          • 9th
          • marital privacy is traditionally protected
          • 14th de process protects privacy M14
          • law is “uncommonly silly” but the Court has no choice but to find it constitutional.
      • Roe v. Wade
        • Roe became pregnant. Pregnant woman fired.
        • Majority opinion
          • M14 due process protects privacy which protects abortion, but this to be baanced against states interests in protecting life of the mother / potentiality of hman life
        • Othsers
          • due process extends proadly beyond legal procedure to include privacy
          • 9th is poper
          • some regulatoin permissible (eg certification of 2 physicians)
          • abortion is a political question ofr legislatres and the public, not a constitutional matter.
      • Planned Parenthood v. Casey, 1992.
        • Pennsylvania requirements for restricting abortion
        • Regulations permissible so long as they dont place undue burden or obstacle on woman seeking abortion
        • Majority:
          • Roe upheld, regulation of abortion permissible below the level of “undue burden”
